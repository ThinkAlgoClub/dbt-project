## 1. What is an Analytics Engineer and Why Does This Problem Exist?

### The Big Picture (No Tech Talk Yet)

Imagine a huge school network: 87,000 students, 140+ schools, across three states (Texas, Ohio, Florida). Every day, things change. Students enroll, transfer schools mid-year, withdraw, or re-enroll.

This data lives in a source system called **PowerSchool** (a Student Information System - SIS).

**Your job as an Analytics Engineer is NOT to just "run a report."**

Your job is to take the *messy, raw, everyday operational data* from PowerSchool and transform it into a *clean, reliable, trustworthy dataset* that anyone in the organization can use to answer questions like "How many students are in Texas?"

### The Core Problem: Raw Data is Ugly

Look at your CSV file. Pretend this is the raw data directly from PowerSchool. An absolute beginner might think, "This looks fine!" But it's full of traps. Let's find them together.

**Real-world example from your CSV:**

Look at student `STU003, Aisha Mohammed`.

| student_id | school_name | entry_date | exit_date | status |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| STU003 | IDEA Quest College Prep | 2025-08-18 | 2025-11-03 | Withdrawn |
| STU003 | IDEA Zenith Academy | 2025-11-04 | (blank) | Active |

**What happened in the real world?**
Aisha started 7th grade at Quest. On Nov 3rd, her family moved to a different neighborhood. She withdrew (exit_code W1). The next day, Nov 4th, she enrolled at Zenith Academy. She is the *same student*, but now she has **two rows** in the raw data.

**The Beginner's Mistake:**
A beginner would count Aisha as **two different students** because they see two rows. This would be wrong. The superintendent would get the wrong enrollment count.

**The Analytics Engineer's Job:**
You must build a model (`dim_students`) that understands Aisha is ONE person who moved between schools. You will track her *current* school and her *history*.

### The Three Layers You Will Build (Part 1 of your interview)

You have to build three things, from raw to useful:

1.  **Staging Model (`stg_sis_enrollments`)** : This is a **clean copy**. You fix the obvious errors.
    - *Example:* In your CSV, row 4 for `Carlos Rodriguez` has `Texas` (spelled out) instead of `TX` (abbreviation). Also, his date is `08-18-2025` (MM-DD-YYYY) instead of `2025-08-18`. You will fix these inconsistencies here.
2.  **Dimension Model (`dim_students`)** : This is a **student master list**. One row per student. It answers "Who is this student, and what is their *current* status?" For Aisha, this table would have ONE row showing she is currently Active at Zenith Academy.
3.  **Fact Model (`fct_enrollments`)** : This is a **history of events**. One row per enrollment period. It answers "When did a student start and end at a specific school?" For Aisha, this table would have TWO rows (one for Quest, one for Zenith).

### The "Grain" (The Most Important Word You'll Learn)

**Grain** means: *What does one single row in my table represent?*

- If you say the grain of `fct_enrollments` is "one row per student," that's wrong (because students can have multiple enrollments).
- If you say the grain is "one row per day per student," that's too much detail.
- **The correct grain for your fact table is: "One row per student per continuous enrollment period at a single school."**

This means a transfer (like Aisha's) creates **two rows**. A student who stays at one school all year creates **one row**.
